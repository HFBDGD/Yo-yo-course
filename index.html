Gear & Setup
Before your first serious throw, you need the right tool. Most people stall because they're fighting gear that works against them.
◎
Learning Goal

By the end of this module, you will have the correct yo-yo for your skill pathway, understand the difference between responsive and unresponsive play, and have your string set to the right length. You will be ready to practise techniques — not fight your equipment.

≡
Explain — The Theory

Yo-yos fall into two categories that define what tricks are possible. A responsive yo-yo has a narrow bearing and returns to your hand with a simple tug — ideal for learning basic throws and classic tricks. An unresponsive yo-yo has a wider bearing and will not return until you perform a trick called a Bind, which allows far more complex string manipulation without the yo-yo snapping back mid-trick.

Your competition goal — the Sport Trick Ladder — requires unresponsive play. But since you already know basic throws, you are at the exact right point to start on a responsive yo-yo, solidify your mechanics, and transition to unresponsive once your Sleeper is consistent. Skipping straight to unresponsive without a clean throw is one of the most common reasons beginners plateau.

String length matters more than most beginners realise. The rule of thumb: standing upright, the yo-yo at the end of an unwound string should reach your belly button. Too long and you'll constantly compensate your throw height; too short and tricks become cramped.

Ideas & Context
Why not start unresponsive?

Unresponsive yo-yos require a Bind to return. Learning to Bind before your throw is clean means two uncertain variables at once. Clean throw first — unresponsive later.

What is a "vibe"?

A vibrating or wobbling yo-yo is called "vibey." Some vibe is normal in cheaper yo-yos. If it wobbles drastically, check the string — a tight or twisted string causes most vibe in beginner yo-yos.

String tension matters

Over time, string twists up and affects trick performance. To fix: let the yo-yo hang free, release the tension, then rewind. Swap string every 2 hours of play.

Core Skill
Throw Mechanics
Everything in yo-yo flows from your throw. A clean, powerful, straight throw is the single most important technical skill in this entire course.
◎
Learning Goal

By the end of this module, you will be able to throw a consistent, straight Sleeper that spins for at least 5 seconds and returns cleanly to your hand. You will also understand the Breakaway throw — the foundation of all competition-level 1A tricks.

≡
Explain — The Theory

The Sleeper is a throw that sends the yo-yo straight down and keeps it spinning at the end of the string — rather than immediately returning. This spinning state at the bottom is called "sleeping." It is the foundation for virtually every 1A trick: Rock the Baby, Trapeze, the Sport Ladder tricks — all require the yo-yo to be sleeping long enough to execute the moves.

The key insight most beginners miss: the yo-yo's spin time is determined almost entirely by the power and straightness of your throw — not by the yo-yo itself. A cheap yo-yo thrown perfectly will outlast an expensive yo-yo thrown lazily. Your goal in this module is to build a throw that is both powerful (fast snap of the wrist) and straight (zero tilt).

The Breakaway is a sideways throw — the yo-yo swings out to the side and across the front of your body rather than straight down. It feels unnatural at first, but the majority of competition 1A tricks — including the Trapeze and Sport Ladder moves — begin from a Breakaway, not a Sleeper. Both throws need to be in your toolkit.

Step-by-Step — The Sleeper
01
Check string orientation
Hold the yo-yo with your palm facing up. The string should come off the top of the yo-yo. If it comes from underneath, flip the yo-yo over. This single check fixes most beginner tilt problems before they start.
→ Wrong orientation = yo-yo spins out sideways every time
02
Make a muscle — the starting position
Raise your throw arm as if flexing a bicep. Elbow up, yo-yo held beside your ear. This coiled position is where all the throw power comes from — don't skip this or you'll get a weak, short-lived spin.
→ The higher the elbow, the more power you can generate
03
Snap straight down — don't follow through
Extend your forearm downward with a sharp wrist snap. Release the yo-yo at the bottom of this motion. Do not swing your full arm — the power comes from the snap, not the swing. Immediately hold your arm still after releasing.
→ Freeze your arm once the yo-yo leaves. Movement causes tilt.
04
Let it sleep — palm stays up
After the throw, keep your palm facing up. This is the difference between the Sleeper and the Gravity Pull — you simply don't tug. The yo-yo will spin at the bottom. Count the seconds. Aim for 5+ before attempting anything else.
→ Palm up = it sleeps. Palm down tug = it returns.
05
Return cleanly — flip and tug gently
To return the yo-yo, rotate your palm to face down and give a light upward tug. You don't need to yank — a gentle tug is enough if the spin is strong. Catch the yo-yo lightly in your palm; don't grab at it.
→ Hard yanking on a weak spin just causes knots

Step-by-Step — The Breakaway
01
Start position — arm out to your dominant side
Unlike the Sleeper where you start with elbow up, the Breakaway begins with your arm extended out to your dominant side (right if right-handed), yo-yo at about shoulder height. Think of it as preparing to throw sideways across your body.
02
Swing across your body
Throw the yo-yo out to your non-dominant side in a smooth arc across the front of your body. The yo-yo should travel in a straight horizontal line, not angled up or down. Imagine guiding it along a rail at shoulder height.
→ Keep the plane of the throw perfectly vertical — this is what stops tilting
03
Let it hang, then return
Once the yo-yo reaches the far side of the arc and begins to hang, tug to return it underneath your catch hand. Catch underhand. The motion should feel like a pendulum arc. This is the exact position all Trapeze and ladder tricks begin from.
→ Tug when the yo-yo reaches shoulder height on the other side
⬡
Ideas & Connections
Why the Breakaway matters more

Over 90% of 1A competition tricks — including every trick on the Sport Ladder above level 3 — begin with a Breakaway, not a Sleeper. The Sleeper is foundational; the Breakaway is operational.

The throw is your "battery"

Every trick you do while the yo-yo is sleeping costs spin time. The more complex the trick, the more spin you need. Judges at competitions see weak throws immediately — the yo-yo dies before the trick is complete.

Practise with a dead yo-yo

For the Sleeper and Breakaway, run through the arm motions with the yo-yo not spinning. Muscle memory for the motion is built separately from the outcome. This technique is used by competitive players for new tricks.

02
Core Skill
Sleeper Control
A yo-yo that tilts, wobbles, or dies too fast isn't a gear problem — it's a technique problem. This module fixes the three issues that kill most beginners' progress.
◎
Learning Goal

By the end of this module, you will be able to correct tilt in a sleeping yo-yo, manage string tension, and consistently achieve 15+ second Sleepers. These are the prerequisites for all picture tricks and Sport Ladder entries.

≡
Explain — The Theory

Three things kill Sleeper quality: tilt (yo-yo leans sideways), string tension (twisted string creates friction), and weak throw power. Each has a specific fix.

Tilt correction: A tilting yo-yo can be nudged back to vertical without catching it. If the yo-yo tilts toward you, gently move your throw hand in the same direction as the tilt to compensate. This takes feel — practise it deliberately rather than waiting for tricks to fail.

String tension: Over time, string twists tighter or looser as you throw and return. Too tight = the yo-yo snaps back unpredictably. Too loose = sloppy trick landings. The fix is the UFO — a sideways throw that deliberately uses string friction to adjust tension. Throw left to loosen; throw right to tighten (for right-handed players).

Step-by-Step — Diagnosing & Fixing Tilt
01
Throw and observe before touching
After your Sleeper throw, resist the urge to immediately fix anything. Watch the yo-yo for 2 seconds. Is it straight? Tilting left? Tilting right? Diagnosing correctly before acting prevents overcorrection.
02
Nudge — don't grab
Move your throw hand slightly in the same direction the yo-yo is tilting. A 1–2cm shift is often enough. The string will apply gentle corrective pressure. If you move too far, you'll cause tilt in the other direction.
→ Think of it as steering, not catching
03
Check string tension before every practice session
Let the yo-yo hang free at the bottom of the string. If it starts slowly spinning on its own, your tension is off. A neutral string hangs completely still. Fix it with the UFO before your session, not mid-trick.
04
The UFO — sideways intentional tilt throw
Throw the yo-yo to the side so it spins at an angle, parallel to the floor. The spinning yo-yo against the string changes tension. Right-hand throw to the right tightens; to the left loosens. Let it hang for 2–3 seconds then tug back.
→ This is also a Sport Ladder trick — you're practising two things at once
⬡
Ideas & Connections
Spin time is your budget

Think of spin time as a budget for tricks. Every string movement spends some of it. A 20-second Sleeper is a big budget — enough for Rock the Baby and back. A 5-second Sleeper is barely enough to start a trick before it dies.

Practise spin time drills

Set a timer. Throw a Sleeper. See how long you can sustain it without letting it return. Do 10 throws in a row and note your average. Track weekly improvement — the numbers will go up faster than you expect.

Dead yo-yo drills

Practice tilt correction with a non-spinning yo-yo hanging on the string. You'll feel the nudge mechanics without time pressure. Transfer this muscle memory to live throws.

Crowd Pleasers
Picture Tricks
Rock the Baby and Eiffel Tower are Sport Ladder tricks #3 and #4. They're also the tricks that make people stop and watch. Learn both here.
◎
Learning Goal

By the end of this module, you will be able to perform Rock the Baby and Eiffel Tower cleanly with a spinning yo-yo — and return cleanly from both. These are Sport Ladder positions 3 and 4, so landing them consistently puts you on the board at any competition.

≡
Explain — The Theory

Picture tricks are string formations — you manipulate the string into a recognisable shape while the yo-yo sleeps. Unlike mount tricks (where the yo-yo lands on the string), picture tricks are all about hand positioning. The critical insight: practise the shape with a dead yo-yo first. The string work is exactly the same whether the yo-yo is spinning or not. Build the hand memory without time pressure, then add the live spin.

Rock the Baby forms a triangular "cradle" with the string, then swings the yo-yo through it like a baby in a hammock. The shape comes from pinching the string correctly with both hands.

Eiffel Tower forms a symmetrical tower shape by threading the string through a loop you create. It's a three-step trick: slingshot position, thread the needle, twist and reveal.

Step-by-Step — Rock the Baby
01
Throw a strong Sleeper
You need at least 10 seconds of spin for this trick. If your Sleeper isn't reliable yet, go back to Module 02. The trick dies if the yo-yo stops spinning before you finish.
02
Grab the string mid-way with your free hand
With your non-throwing hand, grab the string roughly in the middle — about halfway between your throw hand and the yo-yo. Pinch it between thumb and index finger.
03
Pinch the string close to the yo-yo with your throw hand
Now pinch the string just 2–3 inches above the sleeping yo-yo with your throw hand fingers. This creates the top of the cradle.
→ The closer this pinch is to the yo-yo, the larger the cradle
04
Form the cradle — fingers forward and spread
Bring your throw hand forward and point your fingers down and outward. The string between both hands should form a wide triangle — this is the "baby's cradle." Spread your throw hand fingers to make it wider.
05
Rock gently 2–3 times, then release and return
Gently swing the sleeping yo-yo back and forth through the cradle 2–3 times. Then release all string from your non-throw hand and the yo-yo should automatically return from remaining spin energy.
→ If it doesn't return, the spin died — your Sleeper needs to be stronger

Step-by-Step — Eiffel Tower
01
Practise dead first — no spin
Let the yo-yo hang still. Work through all steps below with zero time pressure. You must be able to form the Eiffel Tower shape with a dead yo-yo before attempting it live. This is non-negotiable.
02
Step 1 — The Slingshot
Extend your throw hand forward, thumb out. With your free hand, pinch your thumb and first two fingers together and pull the string through your throw hand — like a slingshot. Keep your throw hand thumb extended throughout.
03
Step 2 — Thread the Needle
Bring your free hand down toward the yo-yo. This creates a small triangle in the string near your throw hand. Take your free hand index finger and pull the string coming off the yo-yo upward through that triangle.
→ Let the rest of the string fall off your free hand fingers once threaded
04
Step 3 — Twist and Reveal
Twist your throw hand forward and point your thumb toward yourself. Pinch the string coming off the yo-yo between your ring finger and middle finger. The Eiffel Tower shape appears. Adjust the string for symmetry if needed.
05
Release and return
Drop all the strings, give a light tug, and catch. If practising live, the whole trick needs to happen within 5–6 seconds of your Sleeper throw — move with deliberate speed once the yo-yo is sleeping.

Ideas & Connections
Sport Ladder positions 3 & 4

In competition, Eiffel Tower is trick #3 and Rock the Baby is trick #4 on the Sport Ladder. Landing both means you've passed the threshold from "can throw" to "can compete." That's a real milestone.

Why dead practice is essential

Picture tricks have no physical "feel" of right vs wrong while the yo-yo is spinning — you can't tell by touch if your hands are positioned correctly. Dead practice builds the spatial memory of where each finger needs to go.

The string triangle tells you everything

In both tricks, when you form the triangle with the string and it looks "off," it means a hand position is wrong. Don't proceed — reset the string and start from step 1. Rushing through a malformed triangle always results in a knot.

Skill Unlock
The Bind
The Bind is the gateway to competition-level play. Master this and you unlock unresponsive yo-yos — longer spin times and the full Sport Ladder.
◎
Learning Goal

By the end of this module, you will be able to perform a reliable frontstyle Bind on an unresponsive yo-yo. This is the single most important technical gateway in all of 1A yo-yo — it separates beginner play from competition play.

≡
Explain — The Theory

An unresponsive yo-yo will not return to your hand with a simple tug — the bearing is too wide for the string to catch. The Bind solves this by manually feeding extra string into the yo-yo gap, creating enough friction for the yo-yo to wind up and return. It sounds complex but the physical motion is quick once learned.

Why bother? Because unresponsive yo-yos spin dramatically longer than responsive ones — 30, 60, even 90+ seconds in competition. This extra spin time is what allows the complex string tricks on the Sport Ladder that simply cannot be done on a short-spinning responsive yo-yo.

There are two primary Binds: Frontstyle (from a Sleeper position) and Sidestyle (from a Breakaway/Trapeze position). Start with Frontstyle as it's more intuitive.

Step-by-Step — Frontstyle Bind
01
Throw a Sleeper on your unresponsive yo-yo
Switch to the wider bearing if you have the YoYoFactory Arrow. Throw a strong Sleeper. Notice that a tug does nothing — the yo-yo stays sleeping. This is the unresponsive state you're learning to work with.
02
Push your free hand index finger into the string
With your free hand index finger, push forward into the string midway between your throw hand and the yo-yo. This creates a loop of string that will feed into the yo-yo gap.
03
Hook the string underneath the yo-yo
Move your throw hand to come around the front of the yo-yo so the yo-yo is now sitting on top of the string. The yo-yo needs to be in the opposite spin direction relative to the string for the Bind to catch.
04
Pinch the string on top — feel the tug
Pinch the string just above the yo-yo with your throw hand fingers. You're about to feed this into the gap. You should feel a light resistance as the string begins winding.
→ If you feel nothing, your loop isn't making contact with the yo-yo gap — adjust
05
Lift throw hand, release free hand — catch
As soon as you feel the tug, pull your throw hand upward while simultaneously releasing the string from your free hand. The yo-yo should shoot back to your hand. Catch with a cupped palm.
→ Timing: lift and release at the exact same moment
⬡
Ideas & Connections
Why the direction matters

The Bind only works when the string loop enters the gap in the opposite direction to the yo-yo's spin. If it's not catching, flip which direction you're feeding the string from. This is the most common reason Binds fail.

The "snag" vs the clean Bind

A snag happens when the yo-yo winds too tightly or a knot forms, causing it to snap back violently during a trick. As you learn to Bind, some snags are normal. They decrease as your technique improves.

The milestone this unlocks

Once you can Bind reliably, you can practise Trapeze, Double or Nothing, and Split the Atom — the core of the Sport Ladder. Those tricks are essentially impossible on a responsive yo-yo without the Bind.

New Tricks — Steps for #5 through #8
#5
Elevator
Throw a Sleeper. Take the string off your throw hand finger and pinch it with your thumb and index finger. Return the yo-yo but as it rises, let go of the pinch — the yo-yo will shoot upward, releasing into the air. Catch it. This trick demonstrates string tension control and clean Sleeper technique.
→ The yo-yo needs strong spin to have enough momentum to complete the upward ride
#6
Brain Twister
Throw a Sleeper. Place your free hand index finger on the string and push it forward into a "front mount" — the yo-yo is now resting on a loop of string between your two index fingers. Now bring your throw hand index finger up around the yo-yo, pushing the string segments to rotate the yo-yo around your free hand finger (one complete somersault). Dismount and return.
→ This is the first "mount" trick — the yo-yo rides on the string itself
#7
Trapeze
Throw a strong Breakaway. As the yo-yo swings across your body, extend your free hand index finger into the path of the string. The yo-yo should swing over your finger and land sleeping on the string on the far side. Hold the position for a moment, then dismount back and Bind to return. This is the foundation of all advanced 1A play.
→ The yo-yo "lands" on the string — aim for the thick part of the string, not the edge
#8
Split the Atom
Start from a Split Bottom Mount (a Sleeper-based mount using both index fingers to split the string). Then perform a pass under the yo-yo with your free hand index finger, push forward into the double string segments, followed by a pass under with your throw hand index finger. Complete 3 somersaults. Dismount and Bind. This is where serious competition play begins.
→ Watch the YoYoTricks.com tutorial for Split Bottom Mount before attempting this

Ideas & Connections
The Trapeze changes everything

The Trapeze is to 1A yo-yo what open chords are to guitar — once you have it, dozens of other tricks become accessible immediately. Every trick above #7 on the Ladder begins from or passes through a Trapeze position.

Practise the sequence, not just tricks

In competition you perform tricks back to back with no rest. Start practising runs of 1→2→3→4 in sequence, then extend to 1→8. The mental flow of the sequence is a separate skill from executing individual tricks.

One miss is allowed

Competition rules give you one missed trick before you're retired. Strategically, this means: if you miss #5 (Elevator), immediately attempt #6 (Brain Twister) and don't panic. Panic causes the second miss.

Q1 — What is the Sport Ladder rule for missed tricks?Reveal ↓
You get one attempt per trick. You are allowed one missed trick — you advance to the next regardless. On your second missed trick, you are retired from the Ladder. The winner is the player who completes the highest trick. Tiebreakers go to the highest trick missed.

Explain — Competition Day Reality

The Sport Ladder at competition is performed in front of judges and an audience. Awards are given to the top performer in each age group (12 and under, 13–17, and 18+), as well as an overall winner for the Ladder. The pressure of being watched changes everything — tricks you can land 9/10 times in your living room suddenly feel uncertain. This is normal and expected.

The antidote is overlearning: practising each trick to the point where it requires no conscious thought. If you're thinking about the steps during a trick, you haven't practised it enough. The goal is automatic execution — your hands know what to do before your brain finishes processing the request.

Equally important is your pre-competition setup: fresh string, correct tension, working yo-yo, and a consistent warm-up routine. Gear failures at competition are almost always preventable.

✓
Competition Day Checklist
Fresh string installed the morning of competition
Old string loses tension responsiveness and snap. Install new polyester string the morning of the event — not the night before.
String tension checked and neutralised
Run 5–10 UFO corrections before your first practice throw. Neutral tension is non-negotiable for clean trick execution.
Backup yo-yo and 3+ spare strings in bag
Strings break. Bearings seize. Carry a fully set-up backup and never leave home without it.
15-minute warm-up done before entering the competition area
Run tricks 1–8 in sequence at least twice in warm-up. Your hands need to be moving before you compete — never go in cold.
Know the competition rules and trick order
Confirm the trick list used at your specific competition — minor regional variations exist. Arrive early enough to watch others compete before your turn.
Mental reset plan for a missed trick
Decide in advance: if you miss a trick, take one breath, shake it off, and move to the next. You have one miss to spend — don't let the miss cause the retirement.

Ideas & Connections
Overlearning is the goal

In performance psychology, "overlearning" means practising past the point of competency to the point of automaticity. For Sport Ladder, aim to land each trick 9/10 times in practice before competition — so that nerves still leave you at 7/10 on the day.

Watching others is learning

Arrive early and watch the other Ladder competitors. You'll see where tricks are commonly missed, how judges evaluate each trick, and whether the specific competition has any format quirks. This is free intelligence.

The goal beyond this course

Once you've competed in the Sport Ladder, the natural next step is entering a Freestyle division — a 2-minute routine set to music using tricks of your own choosing. The Ladder gives you the foundation; Freestyle is where personal style develops.
